How AI Is Used On This Site

The figures below are rough, order-of-magnitude estimates based on public research — not precise measurements of this specific site's usage. Sources are linked at the bottom.

Two different uses of AI here

(1) Building the site — AI coding tools (Claude Code) were used across a handful of sessions to write the code for this project. (2) Running the site — every week, a small, inexpensive LLM reads each venue's events page once and turns the messy HTML into structured show data (band names, dates, times, ticket info). That's one prompt per venue, about 10 prompts total per week.

Why did you use AI to build it?

Honestly: without AI coding tools, this project wouldn't have happened — I don't have a professional software background or the free time to build something like this from scratch. It came together over a handful of sessions with Claude Code after I had the idea, not months of work.

Why use AI to read the venue pages, instead of an API?

None of these venues publish their show calendar as a public feed or API — every single one formats its events page differently (different HTML, different wording, different quirks). A small LLM can read the messy text and pull out "band names, date, time" regardless of layout, instead of me hand-writing and maintaining ten different one-off scrapers that break every time a venue redesigns its site.

What about the environmental cost?

This uses a small, cheap model, run about 10 times a week (~500 times a year) — not a large flagship model, and not continuously. Rough public estimates for similarly-sized modern LLM inference put a single prompt at roughly 0.2–0.3 watt-hours. At that rate, a full year of this site's scraping (~500 prompts) works out to something like 150 watt-hours total — for context, that's less electricity than an hour or two of HD video streaming, for the entire year of runs combined. Water-use estimates for LLM inference are less precise and much-debated, but even generous public estimates for larger-than-necessary models put a year's worth of these prompts at a few gallons — a small fraction of the several hundred gallons of water it takes to produce a single beef burger patty.

I still have concerns about AI

Fair — there are real, serious criticisms of AI at scale: data-center energy and water demand, training-data ethics, labor impact, and reliability. The numbers above are about this one small, specific use case, not a blanket defense of AI generally. If you think this is still the wrong call, email me — colton.lapp@gmail.com.
